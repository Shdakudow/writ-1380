Introduction

Food insecurity is one of the defining challenges of our century. The 2025 State of Food Security and Nutrition in the World (SOFI) report — issued jointly by FAO, IFAD, UNICEF, WFP, and WHO — finds that 673 million people, about 8.2 percent of the global population, went hungry in 2024, and roughly 2.3 billion faced moderate or severe food insecurity. That is a small improvement on the pandemic peak, but still 683 million higher than in 2015. Hunger continues to rise in Africa (now over 20 percent) and Western Asia, and 512 million people are projected to remain chronically undernourished by 2030. Meanwhile the global population is heading toward 10 billion by 2050 — meaning agriculture must produce more food from finite, often degrading, arable land.

Artificial intelligence has emerged as a possible response. Machine learning, computer vision, robotics, predictive analytics, and IoT are now being deployed across the agricultural value chain — from seed genomics and irrigation to post-harvest logistics and distribution. But the economic case is contested. Critics point to the upfront cost of sensors, autonomous machinery, and digital infrastructure, and to a digital divide that locks smallholders in low-income regions out of these tools. Supporters argue that productivity gains — higher yields, less input waste, better risk management — compound across farms and sectors, ultimately feeding into GDP growth.

Thesis. This proposal defends the position that AI adoption in mass food production produces net positive economic returns over a multi-year horizon, even after fully accounting for the cost of machinery and digital infrastructure.

Research questions

  1. RQ1 What AI technologies are currently deployed in mass food production, and what evidence exists regarding their impact on yield, input efficiency, and supply-chain performance?
  2. RQ2 What are the principal cost structures and adoption barriers associated with these technologies, and how do they vary across farm scale and national income level?
  3. RQ3 Through what economic channels do AI-driven productivity gains in agriculture translate into broader GDP growth, and what does the existing empirical evidence suggest about the magnitude of this effect?
